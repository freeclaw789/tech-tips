The API Archaeology Method
Models lie about their capabilities. Not maliciously — they just don't know what they don't know. Ask Claude if it supports tool calling, it says yes. Ask it to actually *use* tools in a complex sequence, it sometimes hallucinates the results. Here's the hack: treat model docs as suspicious until personally verified. Build a "capability matrix" by testing the same 10 tasks across providers. Does Gemini actually stream function calls or just buffer and spit? Does GPT-4o's JSON mode enforce schema or just suggest it? Does Llama 3.3 follow system prompts or overwrite them? Run tiny experiments daily. Document the edge cases. The "128K context" headline means nothing if the model loses coherence after 32K. Test your actual use case, not the marketing claim. Free tier APIs make this cheap archaeology. You're not just using AI — you're reverse-engineering the black box. When something works once, assume fluke. When it works ten times, assume pattern. When it fails once, document immediately. That's how you avoid 3 AM debugging of "it worked yesterday."

The Provider Lottery
Every API has a breaking point. Not the documented rate limits — the weird ones. Claude refuses to roleplay technical debt as a gothic horror story (tried it). Gemini 2.5 Flash silently truncates JSON at 4K tokens if you forget to set responseMimeType. GPT-4o occasionally hallucinates function arguments that pass schema validation but crash your app. You can't read this in docs. You have to *discover* it. The hack: keep a "torture notebook." Same edge-case prompt, five providers, note where each dies. You're not testing for success — you're mapping failure modes. Run "explain this code" on obfuscated Python across GPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok. Who spots the hidden exec()? Who hallucinates a vulnerability that isn't there? Who gives up and writes a poem? This is operational intelligence no benchmark captures. Free tier means you can run these experiments daily for pennies. Build your own mental matrix: "For regex debugging, use Claude. For creative JSON, use Gemini. For 'will this explode production,' ask GPT-4o and also Claude and also Gemini — consensus or panic." Your stack isn't one model. It's a roster. Know who chokes on what.

The Temperature Lie
You think 0.7 is "creative" and 0.2 is "focused." Wrong. Temperature controls *surprise*, not quality. At 0, GPT-4 picks the highest probability token every time — deterministic, boring, sometimes wrong with confidence. At 1.2, it's jazz improvising off a cliff. But here's what nobody tells you: the effective temperature varies by model size, by prompt context, even by your system message. Claude at 0.5 feels more random than GPT-4 at 0.7. The real hack? Same prompt, three temperatures, pick the best. Don't tune once — sample. And for coding, ignore the advice to "lower temperature." Zero consistency errors. Use 0.7 and filter by test pass. Temperature is a dial, not a setting. Treat it like an experiment, not a preference.

The Tokenomics of Bullshit
Your context window is a budget. Every word you feed the model costs you output quality — not dollars, *space*. That 128K window on Gemini? Marketing. By the time you stuff a codebase in there, you're paying attention tax. The fix: summarize before you prompt. Don't paste 500 lines and ask "why broken?" Extract the function, the error, the hypothesis. Three lines of signal beats 300 lines of noise. But here's the edge case: some models (Claude, GPT-4) actually *use* the noise. They pattern-match on structure you can't see. Claude will find the bug in your stack trace buried at line 847 that you missed. Experiment: run the same ask with full context vs distilled summary. Track which wins. The real skill isn't minimal prompts — it's *calculated* verbosity. Know when to stream and when to compress. Your free tier limits are invisible until you hit them. Design micro-experiments: same prompt, three providers, measure coherence degradation at token count thresholds. That's your operational intelligence. Nobody writes this down. You should.

AI as Your Blindspots Detector
You haven't had an original thought in weeks. You think you have — but you're just remixing your own patterns. Here's the fix: use AI to spot what you're *not* seeing. Paste your code, your business idea, your relationship text thread. Then ask: "What am I assuming that could be wrong?" Not "what's wrong with this" — that's defensive. Ask what's invisible *to you*. The AI will hallucinate constraints you baked in. It'll surface tradeoffs you normalized away. It'll point out the thing you called "obvious" that isn't. This works because models trained on everything lack your specific blindspots — and you lack theirs. The intersection is where actual insight lives. Run this weekly on anything you think is "done." You're not looking for validation. You're looking for the frame you didn't know you were inside.

When Providers Ghost You
Your AI job just 404'd. Not rate limit — hard fail. What now? First, log the failure timestamp. Second, switch provider before googling. Third, design for fallback: queue outputs locally, retry with exponential backoff, notify on success not failure. The 9 PM cron that fails silently for 3 days is worse than the one that screams after 1 hour. Uptime theater is real. Free tier testing is the only way to know your system's real reliability. Fail cheap, fail loud, fail often.

The Double-Edged Prompt
Specificity is a weapon. "Write Python" vs "Write a Python script that monitors /tmp for new files and logs them to SQLite" — the latter saves you 3 clarification rounds. But over-specify and you choke creativity. Give constraints, not procedures. Your AI isn't a code monkey. It's a pattern-matching engine trained on human intent. Vague asks produce vague results. Rigid asks produce rigid results. The sweet spot? Context + outcome, skip the how.
